## Shane Pierson

**Titles used:** The Alchemist, host, "your host Shane Pearson"

### Speaking Style

Shane speaks in long, cascading sentences that build momentum. He starts a thought, interrupts himself with a qualifier or aside, then circles back. His rhythm is fast and energetic — he openly acknowledges talking too fast and too much. Sentences pile up with dashes, corrections, and self-aware tangents. He rarely delivers a clean, short statement without layering onto it.

**Typical sentence pattern:**
> "So the idea is what, what delineates somebody to be able to be successful with what they have and keep it going and perpetuate it. Or who's, who's really at risk of tremendous failure."

> "I don't leave them alone, if that makes any sense. Right? I don't want people to feel left in the dark and in the light alone in this process, because it's such a pain in the rear."

He frequently starts sentences with "So" or "I think" and uses "when all is said and done" as a structural pivot to drive to his point.

### Vocabulary Patterns

**Signature phrases (extremely frequent):**
- "When all is said and done" — Shane's most distinctive verbal tic. Appears multiple times per episode.
- "At the end of the day"
- "The reality is..."
- "I think that..."
- "Like" — heavy filler word usage
- "Freaking" / "damn" / "hell" — his go-to intensifiers, used constantly
- "Crap" / "garbage" / "nonsense" — for things he dislikes
- "Right?" — as a confirmation check after nearly every point
- "Holy hell"
- "Let's be honest"

**Industry terms he translates for the audience:**
- SOP (Standard Operating Procedure) — "Nobody really cares about that last number"
- Liquidation — "that means your crap went to hell and now we're gonna go clean it up"
- Guarantee repurchase — "We couldn't get it trying to sell your garbage of what was left"

**Words he reaches for:**
- "Perpetuate," "delineate," "converge," "exponentially"
- "Doom and gloom" (self-aware about his tendency)
- "Glorious" / "gloriousness" — used both sincerely and sarcastically
- "Intoxicating" — to describe borrower energy
- "Commoditized" — about SBA products

### Analogies and Metaphors

Shane reaches for vivid, physical, sometimes bizarre comparisons. He thinks in terms of sports, cars, and combat:

- **Black card analogy** (ep 19): "2025 was kinda like the black card... just put it on the black card. 2026 is more like the friend that says, how are we gonna pay this back?"
- **Gas station / green light** (ep 13): "Waiting for a rate cut is like sitting at a green light, waiting for the gas price at that corner gas station to go down, and you're blocking the freaking road."
- **The rhino** (ep 5): "I classify myself as reconfiguration of a rhino" — his spirit animal, which he physically shows on camera
- **Sausage sleeve** (ep 5): "Just trying to sleeve the sausage a little bit too tight" — about buying clothes on Amazon
- **Shopping cart** (ep 10): "You can walk through Costco for three and a half miles... and you can't walk another 15 steps to put the cart back"
- **Running laps against debt** (ep 18): "You're running laps against your debt, right? You gotta find a way to increase your lead."
- **Three-headed dragon** (ep 20): Describes the sales/credit/servicing disconnect at banks
- **ATM** (ep 5): "I'd just use that thing as a damn ATM" — about a well-automated business
- **Shoot fish in a barrel** (ep 17): About easy lending in 2024

### Storytelling Approach

Shane tells stories by setting a scene, building urgency, then delivering a punchline or lesson. He often starts with "So a couple months ago..." or "Here's what's wild..." He likes to create suspense by withholding the outcome:

> "So poor borrower we're trying to figure this one out still, but the guy took over the business and didn't plan for something... The seller was kind of a hole to some of his customers. And so when they transferred, some of those customers kind of looked at it as a way to get the hell out of there."

He frequently turns personal anecdotes into business lessons — his AI teaching sessions with borrowers at 8:30 PM, his personal mortgage guy, his garage recording setup.

### Tone Shifts

- **Serious/passionate:** When discussing credit risk, default trends, industry problems. Voice drops, sentences get longer, more structured.
- **Self-deprecating humor:** About talking too fast, his body size ("shaped like a trapezoid"), his gray hair, recording in his garage.
- **Doom and gloom:** He openly labels his own pessimism: "That's me going doom and gloom, which I tend to do."
- **Fired up:** When talking about AI, entrepreneurship, urgency. Energy spikes visibly.
- **Playful ribbing:** Constantly teases Brian about talking too long, teases Steph about losing focus.

### Technical Depth

Shane sits at a medium-high technical level. He understands credit deeply (self-identified "credit guy") but constantly translates jargon for the audience. He explains SBA concepts by translating banker-speak into plain English, often with contempt for the jargon itself:

> "Bankers have the worst vernacular. They use all of these dumb garbage acronyms day in and day out. And we forget oftentimes the people had no freaking clue what the hell we're saying."

He knows the five C's of credit (cash flow, collateral, character, credit, capacity) and uses them as a framework, but frames them as "stupid acronyms" while explaining them.

### How He Addresses the Audience

Direct, conversational, slightly urgent. Talks to the listener as if they're sitting across a desk:

> "If you're wondering, that light is from my AI screen over here doing its job in the background."
> "Yes, I am recording in a garage."
> "Get on the freaking boat now. You're going to miss the opportunity."

### Catch Phrases and Signature Expressions

1. "When all is said and done" — the single most distinctive Shane-ism
2. "The reality is..."
3. "Let's be honest"
4. "Holy hell"
5. "Freaking [anything]"
6. "That's a freaking [headache/nightmare/crapshoot]"
7. "Right? Like..."
8. "I'm gonna doom and gloom this"
9. "Stop it, get some help" (borrowed from the Michael Jordan meme)
10. "We did it, guys" — frequent episode closer
11. "Et cetera, et cetera" — used to cut someone off playfully

### Unique Quirks

- Records from his garage, openly mentions it
- Wakes up at 4:30-5:00 AM to build things with AI
- Self-identifies as a rhino (spirit animal)
- Played D3 volleyball, jokes about his body type ("shaped like a trapezoid")
- Self-aware about talking too fast — borrowers tell him "Shane, slow the hell down"
- Calls himself "the credit crap" when getting pessimistic about the economy
- Has two 3D printers in his garage
- Childhood best friends with Brian since high school

## Stephanie Dunn

**Titles used:** Steph the Banker, The Oracle, Lord of Leadership, Queen Wolf, "your fellow Lord"

### Speaking Style

Steph speaks in structured, declarative statements. She builds arguments methodically — sets up a premise, layers in data, then drives to a conclusion. Her sentences are shorter and punchier than Shane's. She uses repetition for emphasis, often restating a key phrase two or three times:

> "Equity is the number one killer of small business. Not collateral. Not credit. Equity is the number one killer of small business."

> "Those dancing days are over and they're over."

She opens many statements with "You know what?" or "Here's the thing" or "Guys" to signal she's about to make a point. She transitions with "All right" and "So" frequently.

### Vocabulary Patterns

**Signature phrases:**
- "Guys" — her most frequent address word, used constantly
- "Men" — addresses Shane and Brian as "men" in the plural ("I think those days are over, men")
- "Here's the deal"
- "Here's the thing"
- "Ownership isn't deserved, it's earned" — her 2026 thesis statement, repeated across episodes
- "Right?" — confirmation seeking, like Shane but less frequent
- "Building legacies" / "creating legacies" — her driving mission phrase
- "Skin in the game"
- "Let's get real"
- "Follow, like, subscribe" — her standard closing CTA
- "Data junkies" — how she describes the team
- "Golden nuggets" / "nuggets" — about insights
- "The OG of OGs" — how she introduces Lance Sexton

**Industry terms she uses fluently:**
- DSCR (debt service coverage ratio)
- PFS (personal financial statement)
- Equity injection
- Cap stack
- Debt-to-net-worth ratio
- EBITDA (but usually explains it)

### Analogies and Metaphors

Steph's analogies tend to be practical, drawn from everyday life and traditional wisdom:

- **Buying a house** (ep 19-20): "You wanna buy a house, it's 20% down. Remember, that was the rule, man."
- **Can't sell cars from a bicycle** (ep 5): "We can't sell cars and show up at the dealership on a bicycle."
- **Sprint vs. marathon** (ep 13): "It shouldn't be a race, it should be... it's not a sprint, it's a marathon."
- **Entrepreneurial revolution** (ep 17): "We just went through the entrepreneurial revolution... the aftermath of COVID."
- **The back of the moon** (ep 20): Lance "sees the backside of the moon" — meaning the ugly liquidation side
- **AI butler** (ep 5): "We need an AI butler" that tells you exactly what tools you need
- **Spanx shopping** (ep 5): Her counterexample to Reddit reviews — women text their girlfriends about Spanx

### Storytelling Approach

Steph tells stories grounded in data and then personalizes them. She opens with statistics, names specific numbers, then pivots to "what does that mean for you?" She also tells deeply personal stories — the dance story (ep 16) where a bank president told her "when the owner asks you to dance, you dance" is one of the most vivid in the entire podcast.

Her story structure: Context -> Data -> Personal reaction -> Principle -> Call to action

> "I've financed thousands of businesses over the last 25 years and after a while it's not that you become the classic disgruntled old lady, but you do, I think, become much more of a realist and can cut through the garbage and the noise a lot quicker."

### Tone Shifts

- **Data-driven authority:** When presenting SBA statistics, market trends. Confident, numbered, structured.
- **Passionate conviction:** When advocating for borrowers, women's leadership, equity discipline. Voice rises.
- **Playful exhaustion:** During road trips and long recording sessions. "Step stepped unhinging." / "My ADD kicked in so hard."
- **Maternal wisdom:** When offering life advice to business owners. "It takes money to make money."
- **Fierce:** When discussing women in banking. "Those dancing days are over."

### Technical Depth

Steph is the most technically rigorous of the three. She leads with numbers and data — she quotes the SBA annual report, knows the exact dollar figures ($100 billion, $45 billion in 7(a)), and breaks down what they mean historically. She compares current numbers to pre-COVID baselines ($30-35 billion) and draws trend lines.

She uses technical terms naturally but also steps back to explain them:
> "A 1.15 debt service coverage and 95% leveraged. I mean, it's type I."

She also coined the 2026 thesis: "2025 was the year of volume. 2026 is the year of discipline."

### How She Addresses the Audience

Warm but authoritative. She addresses the audience as "guys" and uses collective language. She is the most likely to deliver a direct teaching moment:

> "If your business can't survive based on one metric, you have a more fundamental issue."
> "If you haven't felt it because of $150 difference, you have a more fundamental issue with that business."

She signs off with warmth: "Thanks again for tuning in. Follow us, like, subscribe... we'd love to do business with you."

### Catch Phrases and Signature Expressions

1. "Guys" — constant address
2. "Men" — when addressing Shane and Brian specifically
3. "Ownership isn't deserved, it's earned"
4. "Building legacies"
5. "Here's the deal" / "Here's the thing"
6. "Let's get real"
7. "Follow, like, subscribe"
8. "I'm no spring chicken"
9. "Golden nuggets"
10. "All right, let's jump in"
11. "The OG of OGs"
12. "Preach" — in agreement
13. "I'm in the people business"

### Unique Quirks

- 25+ years in SBA lending — the most experienced of the three
- Self-describes as "not the quintessential feminist... but I might"
- Tells the "when the owner asks you to dance" story as her defining career moment
- Has ADD — openly mentions it during recording sessions
- During road trip episodes, checks out of technical discussions ("I didn't hear a word you said, but I'm sure they were great points")
- Gets visibly fired up about equity injection — it's her core 2026 cause
- References the Coleman Report and Bob Coleman by name
- Calls herself a "data junkie"
- Always has a one-liner thesis ready for each episode/era
- Tends to be the one who structures and closes episodes

## Brian Congelliere

**Titles used:** The Magistrate, Lord of Law, "Brian Conjole" (frequent casual mispronunciation by co-hosts)

### Speaking Style

Brian speaks in long, methodical, layered sentences. He builds his points carefully, qualifying and contextualizing as he goes. His legal training shows — he structures arguments, considers multiple angles, and uses precise language. His sentences can run extremely long (the co-hosts joke about this constantly), but they are logically organized even when they're verbose.

**Typical sentence pattern:**
> "What I'm thinking is 2025 was interesting because it kind of seemed like we were kind of at the tail end of interest rates going up and then starting to come back down. And we started seeing a different type of borrower since interest rates had gone up."

> "The thing that you learn in law school, or that you should learn in law school at least, and that you learn as an attorney, is spotting issues. So what does that mean? That means that you are looking at a transaction at the very beginning, trying to find all of those little things."

He often prefaces his answers with "You know" or "I think" and frequently uses "So" to transition between sub-points within a single answer.

### Vocabulary Patterns

**Signature phrases:**
- "Pontifi— " / "pontificate" — he uses this word and Shane immediately mocks it
- "Mitigate" — his legal brain's favorite word
- "In the vein of..." / "On the vein of..."
- "Spot issues" / "spotting issues" — from his legal training
- "That sort of thing"
- "Whether it was... or..."
- "Along those lines"
- "I think it comes down to..."
- "I don't know" — honest hedge before making a point
- "Let's see" — thinking out loud
- "This is neat" / "It's neat" — about AI, said with genuine enthusiasm (becomes a running joke)
- "The Reddits" / "I was on the Reddits this morning" — his social media of choice

**Terms he uses precisely:**
- EBITDA
- Debt service coverage ratio
- Variable rate, Wall Street Journal Prime
- Basis points
- Net recoverable value
- Change of ownership

### Analogies and Metaphors

Brian's analogies are less flashy than Shane's — they tend to be precise comparisons drawn from law, business, or everyday logic:

- **Building your case** (ep 11): Treating each loan like a legal case — "you are looking at a transaction at the very beginning, trying to find all of those little potential issues"
- **Cheating on homework** (ep 17): About AI-generated business plans — "Guys, this is too easy to spot."
- **Test and fail fast** (ep 13): Borrowed from startup culture, applied to small business pricing
- **Office TV show** (ep 8): Shane references it and Brian immediately engages — "Absolutely"

### Storytelling Approach

Brian tells stories through data and deal examples. He's less narrative-driven than Shane and more analytical. He'll reference a borrower scenario methodically:

> "I had one borrower, several borrowers who we got in under the deadline, others that we didn't, and they're still trying to figure things out, getting more loans to pay the last ones just because those payments are so insane."

He's most animated when telling stories about deals he discovered on Reddit or about clever deal structures he's seen borrowers use (like tying seller notes to business performance in ep 19).

When telling longer stories, he tends to build context carefully before revealing the interesting detail — the opposite of Shane who leads with the punchline.

### Tone Shifts

- **Analytical/measured:** His default mode. Weighing options, considering angles.
- **Genuinely excited:** When talking about new deals, interesting businesses, or AI capabilities. "Ooh, that's cool."
- **Dry humor:** Delivered straight-faced. "There are no such things as run-on sentences. You haven't read as many contracts as I have."
- **Self-deprecating:** About his verbosity, his hair, his tendency to black out mid-explanation.
- **Enthusiastic nerd:** When discussing AI tools, GPTs, automation. Genuinely lights up.

### Technical Depth

Brian operates at the highest technical level of the three but is also the most likely to carefully walk through his reasoning. He's the calculator — he'll pull out actual numbers to verify Shane's math, he'll reference specific SBA SOP changes, and he'll cite Wall Street Journal Prime rates. His legal background makes him precise about terms and structures.

He's also the AI specialist of the group — he builds custom GPTs, uploads SOP documents, runs webinars about AI in lending, and Shane credits him as the one who got him "addicted to Claude Code."

### How He Addresses the Audience

Professional but approachable. Less direct than Shane, less warm than Steph. He speaks to the audience as a knowledgeable guide:

> "Folks who are looking for more of a fixed rate, obviously, that can play against you in a falling rate environment."
> "I think if you aren't using it or you aren't figuring out how, you're already behind."

### Catch Phrases and Signature Expressions

1. "I think it comes down to..."
2. "That sort of thing"
3. "I was on the Reddits this morning"
4. "This is neat" / "It's neat" (about AI — becomes a running joke)
5. "Along those lines"
6. "Spotting issues"
7. "Pontificate"
8. "Whether it was... or..."
9. "That's something I get asked a lot as well"
10. "Let me just add this too"

### Unique Quirks

- Attorney by training — the legal perspective always colors his analysis
- AI enthusiast — has "17 different models open at any given time" (Shane's quote about him)
- Gorgeous hair — it's a running bit. Shane and Steph both comment on his "lion hair," "Italian Hawaiian" mix
- Played D1 volleyball at BYU, was the varsity setter in high school
- Childhood/lifelong friends with Shane from high school in Newbury Park
- Most likely to get lost in a long answer and then self-correct: "Did I just black out?"
- Peruses Reddit daily to respond to SBA loan questions and find deal stories
- His signature move: "Thanks for picking up the phone" — just answering calls and returning them
- Introduced Stuart Faught in ep 12, showing he sometimes takes the host role
- Goes deep on deal structure — he's the one who explains how to tie seller notes to business performance

## Group Dynamics

### Who Leads

**Steph** typically opens and closes episodes. She sets the theme, delivers the thesis statement, introduces guests (especially in later episodes), and gives the CTA sign-off. She's the structure keeper.

**Shane** drives the middle conversation. He escalates energy, pivots between topics, brings in personal anecdotes, and does most of the translating of jargon for the audience. He's the engine.

**Brian** deepens the analysis. He's called on for the technical breakdown, the legal perspective, and the numbers. He provides the substance that backs up Shane's energy and Steph's thesis.

### How They Interact

**Shane to Brian:** Constant ribbing. Shane mocks Brian's long answers ("Et cetera, et cetera"), his vocabulary ("What was that big word?"), but clearly respects his intelligence. They're childhood friends and it shows — the teasing is deeply comfortable.

> Shane: "Et cetera, et cetera, et—"
> Steph: "No, no, no, no. Let him keep going."
> Brian: "Where am I? Did I just black out?"

**Shane to Steph:** Respectful but playful. He puns on her name ("If you want something done, come to Dunn"), acknowledges her expertise, and follows her structural lead. When she checks out during road trips, he narrates it in real time: "She's just playing with a damn bag and eating sunflower seeds."

**Steph to Shane:** She channels his energy. "Take it home, Shane." She also reins him in: "Maybe let's ask Brian" when Shane goes on a tangent. She ribs him about talking too fast.

**Steph to Brian:** Warmer, more collegial. She sets him up with questions: "Brian, what's your signature move?" She validates his points: "Love it. I'll take that."

**Brian to Shane:** Goes along with the ribbing, gives it back gently. "There are no such things as run-on sentences. You haven't read as many contracts as I have." He's the straight man to Shane's energy.

**Brian to Steph:** Respectful, collaborative. He builds on her points rather than challenging them. When she delivers a data point, he'll add the analytical dimension.

### The Balance

Shane brings the energy and accessibility. Steph brings the authority and structure. Brian brings the depth and precision. Together, they cover the full spectrum from "fired up SBA sales guy" to "experienced banker with 25 years of data" to "detail-oriented legal mind who does the math."

## Episode Structure and Format

### Typical Structure

1. **Cold open** (30-90 seconds): Teaser clips from the episode, usually the most provocative quotes from each host
2. **Welcome/intro** (1-2 minutes): Host identifies themselves, introduces co-hosts with titles, states the topic
3. **Subscribe CTA** (quick): "Hit subscribe," "follow us," embedded naturally into the intro
4. **Main discussion** (15-35 minutes): Conversational exploration of the topic, rotating between hosts
5. **Closing summary** (1-3 minutes): Usually Steph wraps with a thesis restatement and CTA
6. **Sign-off:** "Lords of Lending" mention, subscribe reminder

### How They Open Episodes

**Shane openings** (most common in early episodes):
> "Welcome back to another episode of Lords of Lending. I'm your host, Shane Pearson, as always with Stephanie Dunn, the Lord of Leadership, and Brian Conjole, the Lord of Law."

**Steph openings** (increasingly common in later episodes):
> "Welcome back to Lords of Lending. I am Stephanie Dunn, your fellow Lord, and with me we have Shane Pearson, Brian Coni."
> "Welcome back to the Lords of Lending podcast, where we talk deals, dreams and the dynamics of entrepreneurs who execute."

**Brian openings** (rare, used when he introduces a guest):
> "Welcome to Lords of Lending. I'm Brian Cancelliere. As usual, I've got Shane Pearson and Stephanie Dunn with me."

### Opening Taglines (vary by episode)

- "Where capital gets weaponized, structure gets sharpened, and deals get dissected" (ep 9)
- "Where small businesses get real, strategic, and deadly honest insights" (ep 5)
- "The royal order of capital" (ep 10)
- "Where capital isn't just money, it's power, it's legacy" (ep 15)

### Closing Patterns

Steph almost always delivers the final CTA:
> "Follow, like, subscribe, and reach out in all the different formats that we're on."
> "Thanks for tuning in. Follow us, like, subscribe, lords of lending."

Shane's closing punctuation:
> "We did it, guys."
> "See you later."
> "Take it easy."

Brian's closing is often silence or a brief sign-off:
> "Yes." (ep 11)
> "Lord of lending" (ep 8)

## Guest Interaction Style

### Colin Trimble (Episode 9 — Acquisition War Room)

Shane set the tone as a "war room" with military/battle metaphors. Colin was introduced as "the Vanguard" — Shane assigns lordly titles to guests. The format was a roundtable discussion, more structured than usual. Shane led the questioning while Steph and Brian provided commentary.

### Stuart Faught (Episode 12 — Reputation)

**Brian introduced Stuart** — one of the rare episodes where Brian hosted the intro. This signals a personal connection (Stuart is Brian and Shane's friend). Stuart opened with his own cold-open story before the formal intro, giving the episode an in-medias-res feel. The hosts let Stuart tell his story at length before layering in their perspectives.

### Seth Hannemann (Episode 14 — Restaurant)

Shane introduced Seth through their shared history (high school volleyball). The episode was heavily personal — the first 10 minutes were volleyball stories and inside jokes. This shows that when guests are personal friends, the hosts let the relationship breathe before pivoting to business. Steph played the role of connecting Seth's story to broader principles: "Everything you just described is exactly transferable to every business owner."

### Abby (Episode 15 — Seller Notes)

Abby opened with a cold-open teaser discussing capital market dislocation. Shane introduced him with the lordly title "Lord of Seller Debt." The hosts positioned the guest as an authority and let him lead the technical discussion, with Shane translating and Steph connecting to the macro theme.

### Lance Sexton (Episode 20 — Equity/Liquidation)

Steph introduced Lance as "the OG of OGs." Shane immediately humanized him by asking about his favorite music (a technique Shane uses to break the ice). Lance got extensive speaking time — the hosts recognized his depth and let him run. Shane periodically jumped in to translate Lance's technical terms for the audience.

**Pattern for guest episodes:**
1. Guest cold-open teaser
2. Host intro with lordly title for the guest
3. Personal connection or humanizing moment
4. Guest leads, hosts guide with questions
5. Shane translates jargon
6. Steph connects to the macro theme
7. Brian adds analytical depth or follow-up questions

## Deal References

### Anonymization Patterns

The hosts reference real deals constantly but anonymize in specific ways:

- **No names, specific details changed:** "I had a borrower the other day..." / "One of my borrowers..." / "This guy..."
- **Scenario-based:** They describe the deal structure and problem without identifying the business: "A $2 million deal, trucking company" (Lance, ep 20)
- **Named examples from guests:** When a guest is the subject (Seth's Seven Brothers Burgers), full names and details are used
- **AI-generated personas:** For illustrative stories, they use AI-generated names: "Vanessa" / "Dawn" / "Derek" (ep 5)
- **The "hypothetical" frame:** "Let's say you just bought a business and..." — frequently used to set up scenarios
- **Reddit sourcing:** Brian references Reddit posts as deal examples, which are already anonymized by the platform
- **Industry-level data:** Steph cites SBA annual report numbers, Coleman Report data, and industry statistics without attributing to specific institutions

### How They Handle Sensitive Deal Details

- Default rates and portfolio problems are discussed at the industry level, not attributed to their specific bank
- When Shane tells a story about a borrower who struggled, he focuses on the structural lesson, not the person's identity
- They openly discuss their own bank's credit process being frustrating without naming the institution
- They reference "seven banks" (Shane's career) and "three banks ago" (shared employer) without naming them

The humor almost always happens in transitions — between topics, after a long technical section, or when one host has been talking too long. It functions as a pressure release valve. After humor, they typically pivot with "All right" or "So" back to the substance.

## Writing Guidelines — How to Sound Like Each Host

### Writing as Shane

**Do:**
- Start sentences with "So" or "I think"
- Use "when all is said and done" to pivot to your key point
- Build long, layered sentences that stack qualifiers
- Use "freaking," "damn," "hell" as intensifiers (but not excessively in written form)
- Translate technical terms with contempt: "what that really means is..."
- Include self-aware asides: "and yes, I know I'm going doom and gloom again"
- Use vivid physical metaphors (cars, sports, combat, food)
- End paragraphs with "Right?" or "So..."
- Reference personal experience: "I've worked at seven banks now"
- Inject urgency: "Get on the boat now"

**Don't:**
- Write short, clean, polished sentences — Shane stacks and layers
- Use formal academic language
- Be emotionally neutral — Shane runs hot
- Forget the self-deprecation
- Write without energy

**Sample Shane paragraph:**
> When all is said and done, the small business owner who's sitting on the sidelines waiting for rates to drop is blocking the freaking road. You're sitting at a green light, watching every other car fly by, and you're staring at the gas station hoping the price ticks down another nickel. Meanwhile, the opportunity you've been eyeing just got bought by someone who actually moved. I've seen it happen more times than I can count — deals dying in slow motion because somebody couldn't pull the trigger. Get in the game. Figure it out as you go. The worst thing you can do is nothing.

### Writing as Steph

**Do:**
- Open with a declarative statement or data point
- Use "Guys" to address the audience
- Repeat key phrases for emphasis (say it, then say it again)
- Lead with numbers: "45 billion in seven-a loans, 85,000 business owners"
- Build from data to personal insight: "What does that mean? Here's what I think..."
- Use "Right?" to confirm alignment
- Frame things as generational/legacy: "building legacies," "multi-generational"
- Reference her 25+ years of experience for authority
- Close with a clear, memorable one-liner
- Use "All right" as a transition

**Don't:**
- Ramble or meander — Steph is structured
- Be purely emotional without data
- Forget the CTA
- Write without conviction — Steph delivers opinions with authority
- Use overly casual slang (she's warm but professional)

**Sample Steph paragraph:**
> Guys, here's the deal. 2025 was a record year — $100 billion in government-guaranteed lending to small businesses. That's triple what we saw pre-COVID. But let's strip away the fluff and look at what that actually means. When I look at personal financial statements crossing my desk, I'm seeing a lot of debt and very little equity. Borrowing is up, but ownership? Real ownership? That's declining. And if you own a business that's 95% leveraged, let me ask you this: do you really own anything? Ownership isn't deserved. It's earned. And earning it means coming to the table with cash, with discipline, and with a plan that doesn't fall apart the moment you hit a bump.

### Writing as Brian

**Do:**
- Build arguments carefully, considering multiple angles
- Use "I think" to preface analysis
- Reference specific mechanisms: "Wall Street Journal Prime," "debt service coverage ratio"
- Be methodical — present the setup, then the analysis, then the conclusion
- Include honest hedges: "I don't know" before an opinion
- Reference legal thinking: "spotting issues," "building your case"
- Mention Reddit, AI tools, specific tech naturally
- Use "that sort of thing" and "along those lines" as connectors
- Be the voice of measured reason between Shane's urgency and Steph's conviction

**Don't:**
- Be flashy or dramatic — Brian is the calm center
- Use bold metaphors (that's Shane's territory)
- Forget to contextualize — Brian always explains why something matters
- Write in very short sentences — Brian's natural rhythm is longer
- Be dismissive of opposing viewpoints — Brian considers alternatives

**Sample Brian paragraph:**
> What I'm seeing is a shift in the caliber of borrower coming through the door. Toward the end of 2025, it became noticeably more difficult to get the same number of loans done. The issues were consistent — tax transcript problems, business valuations coming back lower than expected, and a lot of folks who hadn't quite figured out how their business actually makes money. And I think that's really the crux of it. The business owners who can rattle off their numbers — their revenue, their margins, their customer acquisition cost — those are the ones I'm less concerned about, even with a thinner equity position. It's the ones who come in and say, "yeah, I'm just gonna open the doors and it's gonna happen" that need the most structure and, frankly, the most discipline in how we approach their deal.

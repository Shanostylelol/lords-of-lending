# How to Build a $10M SBA Pipeline in 12 Months

**Author:** Shane

Let me hit you with some math before we get into the how. A $10 million SBA pipeline sounds like a big number. It sounds like something a seasoned originator builds over years of relationship grinding. It's not. It's 15 to 20 deals at an average size of $500,000 to $700,000. That's roughly one to two new qualified deals entering your pipeline every month. One or two. That's it. The number stops being scary when you break it down, right?

The problem isn't that $10M is an unrealistic target. The problem is that most originators don't have a system. They've got a contact list, maybe a CRM they log into once a week, and a vague sense that they should "do more outreach." That's not a pipeline. That's a hope. And hope is not a strategy — it's what you cling to when you don't have one.

I've built pipelines from scratch multiple times across [seven banks](/how-to-become-sba-loan-broker). Some were in markets I knew. Some were in cities where I didn't know a single person. The system is the same either way. Here's how it actually works, month by month.

## Month-by-Month Action Plan

### Months 1-3: Foundation

This is where most people skip ahead and pay for it later. The first three months are not about closing deals. They're about building the infrastructure that makes deals possible.

**Month 1:** Set up your CRM (HubSpot free tier is fine — stop overthinking this). Build your pipeline stages: Lead, Qualified, Gathering Docs, Submitted to Lender, In Underwriting, Approved, Closing, Funded. Create email templates for initial outreach, follow-up, and document requests. Identify your first 3 lender relationships and have introductory calls. Learn their credit boxes cold.

**Month 2:** Begin outreach to your top 3 referral source categories (covered below). Target 20 meaningful conversations this month — not cold emails, actual conversations. Attend one local business event. Post twice on LinkedIn about SBA lending (not salesy garbage — actual insights).

**Month 3:** Your pipeline should have 5-10 leads in various stages of qualification. Follow up on every single one. Start tracking conversion rates: how many leads become qualified deals, how many qualified deals get submitted, how many submissions become approvals. These numbers will tell you everything about where your pipeline leaks.

### Months 4-6: Acceleration

By now you have a rhythm. You know which lead sources respond, which lender partners are fast, and what kinds of deals fit your strengths.

**Double down on what's working.** If business brokers are sending you deals, take every business broker in your market to coffee. If CPAs are referring, build a CPA referral program. If LinkedIn is generating inbound inquiries, post three times a week instead of two.

**Submit your first 3-5 deals to lenders.** Even if some don't close, the process teaches you more than any training can. You'll learn what underwriters push back on, what document gaps slow things down, and how to [structure deals that survive committee](/sba-deal-structuring-guide).

**Target: 8-12 deals in pipeline, 3-5 actively in underwriting.**

### Months 7-12: Compounding

This is where the flywheel kicks in. Closed deals generate referrals. Lender relationships deepen. Your reputation in the market starts to mean something. The deals start coming to you instead of you chasing every one.

**Target by month 12: 15-20 deals in pipeline at various stages, $8-12M total pipeline value, 4-6 funded deals behind you.**

For the detailed framework on [building an SBA lending pipeline](/building-sba-lending-pipeline) stage by stage, we've laid out the full operational model separately.

If you hit those numbers, you've built a pipeline that will sustain a real practice. If you've blown past them, you're ready to start thinking about [scaling into a brokerage](/bank-bdo-to-independent).

## Lead Sources Ranked by Conversion Rate

Not all leads are created equal. After years of tracking this, here's how I rank lead sources from highest conversion to lowest:

**Tier 1 — Highest conversion:**
- **Business brokers and M&A advisors** — These people have qualified sellers with real businesses at real prices. The buyer is already identified. You're coming in to finance a deal that's largely structured. Conversion rate: 25-40%.
- **CPA and attorney referrals** — Their clients trust them. When a CPA says "call this SBA guy," the borrower is warm from the first conversation. Conversion rate: 20-35%.
- **Repeat borrowers** — Someone you've already closed for. They know the process, they trust you, and they have a new opportunity. Conversion rate: 40%+.

**Tier 2 — Moderate conversion:**
- **LinkedIn inbound** — People who find your content and reach out. They're pre-educated and self-selected. Conversion rate: 15-25%.
- **SBA LINC (Lender Match)** — The SBA's own referral tool. Volume is there but quality varies. Conversion rate: 5-15%.
- **Networking events and BNI groups** — Slow burn, but the relationships compound. Conversion rate on direct referrals: 15-25%.

**Tier 3 — Volume play, lower conversion:**
- **Cold outreach** to business owners via email or LinkedIn. You're interrupting someone who wasn't thinking about SBA financing. Conversion rate: 2-5%.
- **Paid digital leads** — Google Ads, Facebook. Expensive, and the borrowers are often early-stage tire-kickers who are 6-18 months from a real transaction.

The lesson: spend 80% of your relationship-building time on Tier 1 sources. They close faster, they're more pleasant to work with, and they generate referrals. For a full breakdown of [how to source SBA loans](/how-to-source-sba-loans) across all channels, we've got a separate guide on that.

## The Follow-Up System That Actually Works

I cannot freaking stress this enough: follow-up is where pipelines live or die. Most originators follow up once, maybe twice, then move on. That's insane. The data across every sales profession shows that most conversions happen between the 5th and 12th contact. SBA lending is no different.

Here's the system I use:

- **Day 1:** Initial conversation. Send a recap email within 2 hours.
- **Day 3:** Follow up with a relevant resource (article, rate update, comparable deal example).
- **Day 7:** Check in. "Hey, just following up — have you had a chance to pull together those tax returns?"
- **Day 14:** If no response, change the angle. Share something of value that isn't about their deal.
- **Day 30:** Final direct follow-up. "I want to make sure this is still on your radar. If timing has changed, no worries — just let me know."
- **Monthly after that:** Add them to a nurture sequence. Market updates, podcast episodes, relevant articles. Stay visible without being pushy.

Log every touch in your CRM. Every single one. When that person calls you six months later saying "I'm ready," you'll know exactly where you left off.

## How to Qualify a Deal in 10 Minutes

Time is the only resource you can't make more of. Spending three hours on a call with a borrower who has no equity injection and a business that does 2x debt service on a good day is three hours you'll never get back. Learn to qualify fast.

My 10-minute qualification framework:

1. **What's the deal?** Acquisition, expansion, refinance? What's the purchase price or loan amount?
2. **What's the equity injection?** Where is it coming from? Is it verified?
3. **What's the DSCR?** Quick napkin math: business cash flow divided by estimated annual debt service. Is it above 1.2x?
4. **Is the business SBA-eligible?** Check the NAICS code, check size standards, check for ineligible activities.
5. **Does the borrower have relevant experience?** If they're buying a manufacturing company and they've been a barista for 10 years, we've got a problem.

If three of those five check out, the deal is worth a deeper look. If two or more are red flags, have the honest conversation now — not after you've invested 40 hours. This qualification skill is one of the things that separates originators who earn from originators who spin their wheels.

## Why Most Pipelines Die (and How to Prevent It)

When all is said and done, pipelines don't die from a lack of leads. They die from a lack of follow-through. Here are the three killers I've seen take down more originators than bad credit scores:

**1. No system.** Leads come in, get worked for a week, and then get buried under the next shiny opportunity. Without a CRM and a follow-up cadence, you're relying on memory. Memory fails.

**2. No qualification discipline.** You spend all your time on deals that were never going to close, and the good deals in your pipeline don't get the attention they need. Learn to say no fast so you can say yes to the right deals.

**3. No patience.** SBA deals take 60-120 days from application to funding. If you started building your pipeline in January, your first closing might not happen until April or May. Most people quit at month 3 because they haven't made money yet. The ones who push through to month 6 are the ones who build careers.

## FAQ

### How many deals does it take to build a $10M SBA pipeline?

At an average deal size of $500,000-$700,000, you need approximately 15-20 deals in various stages. That means adding 1-2 new qualified opportunities per month for 12 months while maintaining and progressing existing deals through the pipeline.

### What's the best lead source for new SBA originators?

Business brokers and M&A advisors. They have qualified buyers and sellers, the deals are partially structured, and the conversion rates are the highest of any lead source at 25-40%. Start by identifying every business broker in your market and scheduling introductory meetings.

### How long does it take to close an SBA deal?

From application to funding, typical SBA 7(a) deals take 60-120 days. Deals involving real estate or complex structures can take longer. The documentation collection phase — getting everything you need from the borrower — is usually the biggest variable. Having a [solid documentation system](/sba-application-checklist) compresses this significantly.

### What CRM should I use for SBA pipeline tracking?

HubSpot's free tier is the best starting point for most independent originators. It handles contacts, deal pipelines, email tracking, and basic reporting without costing anything. Upgrade to Salesforce when you're doing enough volume to justify the investment and need custom SBA-specific fields.

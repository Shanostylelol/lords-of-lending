# SBA Lending 101: The Foundation Every Originator Needs

Guys, here's the deal. If you're getting into SBA lending and you don't understand what the SBA actually does, you're going to waste months chasing deals that were never going to close. I've been in this space for over 25 years, and the number one thing I see with new originators is the same thing I see with new borrowers: they think the SBA is a bank. It's not. And once you understand what it actually is, everything else starts to click.

This is the foundation. You cannot skip it. Every deal you'll ever touch — acquisitions, expansions, working capital, partner buyouts — runs through the same set of rules and the same network. The originators who learn this first are the ones who stop spinning their wheels and start closing. The ones who skip it are the ones calling me six months later asking why their deals keep dying.

## What the SBA Actually Does (And What It Doesn't)

The Small Business Administration does not lend money. I'm going to say that again because it matters. The SBA does not make loans. It guarantees them.

Here's how that works. A borrower walks into a bank and asks for a business loan. The bank looks at the deal and says, "This is too risky for us to do on our own. There's not enough collateral, or the business is too new, or the industry is too volatile." Under normal circumstances, that deal gets declined.

But the SBA steps in and says to the bank: "We'll guarantee a portion of this loan. If the borrower defaults and you followed our rules, we'll cover 75% to 85% of your loss." That guarantee is the entire mechanism. It transfers risk from the lender to the federal government, which makes the lender willing to approve deals it would otherwise reject.

The borrower never interacts with the SBA directly during the loan process. There's no SBA office where you submit your application. You apply through an SBA-approved lender — a bank, a credit union, a CDFI — and that lender handles the origination, underwriting, and closing. The SBA's role is to set the rules, provide the guarantee, and collect a guarantee fee.

This distinction is everything. When a borrower says "I got an SBA loan," what they really mean is "I got a bank loan that was partially guaranteed by the SBA." That guarantee is what made it possible.

## The 7(a) Program Explained

The 7(a) is the SBA's flagship loan program. It accounted for over $45 billion in approved lending in 2025 — the highest volume in the program's history. It's the program you'll work with most as an originator, and understanding it cold is non-negotiable.

Here are the basics every originator needs to have memorized:

- **Maximum loan amount:** $5 million
- **Guarantee percentage:** 85% for loans up to $150,000; 75% for loans above $150,000
- **Interest rates:** Variable, tied to the Wall Street Journal Prime Rate plus a spread (capped by the SBA based on loan size)
- **Maximum terms:** 25 years for real estate, 10 years for equipment and working capital, up to 10 years for business acquisitions
- **Equity injection:** Minimum 10% for change-of-ownership transactions (many lenders require more)
- **Use of proceeds:** Working capital, equipment, acquisitions, real estate, debt refinancing, leasehold improvements, partner buyouts, franchise purchases

The 7(a) is flexible. That flexibility is one of its greatest strengths and one of its greatest sources of confusion. A single program covers everything from a $75,000 working capital line for a startup to a $5 million acquisition of an established business. The rules change based on the deal type, the loan size, and the lender's own credit criteria.

The SBA Express program is a subset of the 7(a). It caps at $500,000 with a 50% guarantee and offers faster turnaround for simpler deals. Most originators start encountering Express deals early because the smaller size makes them less intimidating to package.

## The SBA Lending Network: Lenders, CDCs, CDFIs, and PLP

This is the part that trips people up. The SBA lending world isn't one monolithic thing. It's a network of different types of institutions, each with their own role, their own appetite, and their own quirks. As an originator, knowing who does what — and who to call for which deal type — is half the job.

**SBA-Approved Lenders.** These are banks and credit unions that have been authorized by the SBA to originate 7(a) loans. Not every bank does SBA lending. The ones that do range from massive national lenders processing thousands of SBA loans per year to small community banks that do a handful.

**PLP Lenders (Preferred Lender Program).** PLP lenders have been granted delegated authority by the SBA to approve loans in-house. They don't have to send the application to the SBA for a separate review. This speeds up the approval process significantly — sometimes by weeks. Most experienced originators prefer working with PLP lenders because of that speed advantage. When a deal is time-sensitive, and most acquisition deals are, getting a PLP approval can be the difference between closing and losing the deal to another buyer.

**CDFIs (Community Development Financial Institutions).** CDFIs are mission-driven lenders that focus on underserved communities and borrowers who may not qualify at traditional banks. They tend to have more flexible credit criteria and may accept deals that conventional SBA lenders pass on. CDFIs are important to know about because some of the best deals — the ones where a borrower has strong experience and solid cash flow but a thin credit profile — find their home at a CDFI.

**CDCs (Certified Development Companies).** CDCs are the entities that handle SBA 504 loans, which focus on commercial real estate and heavy equipment. While the 504 isn't a 7(a) product, you'll encounter it frequently, and understanding the relationship between CDCs and the 504 program helps you steer borrowers to the right product.

## How Deals Flow: From Borrower to Approval

Here's the lifecycle of an SBA deal, simplified to the essentials every originator needs to understand on day one.

**Step 1: Borrower inquiry.** A business owner contacts you (or a lender directly) about financing. This might be a startup looking for working capital, an entrepreneur looking to buy an existing business, or an established owner looking to expand or refinance.

**Step 2: Initial qualification.** This is your first filter. Does this deal make basic SBA sense? Is the business eligible? Is the borrower eligible? Is the loan purpose eligible? Does the deal have enough cash flow to service the debt? You should be able to answer these questions in the first conversation. If you can't, you're going to waste weeks on deals that go nowhere.

**Step 3: Loan packaging.** You assemble the loan package — the LOI (for acquisitions), business financials, personal financial statements, tax returns, business plan, and all required SBA forms. The quality of this package matters enormously. A clean, well-organized package signals competence to the lender. A messy one raises doubts before they even look at the numbers.

**Step 4: Lender submission.** You submit the package to an appropriate lender. And "appropriate" is the key word. Not every lender wants every deal. An originator who sends a startup deal to a lender that only does acquisitions is burning credibility.

**Step 5: Underwriting.** The lender's credit team reviews the package, evaluates the risk, and makes a decision. At PLP lenders, this happens internally. At non-PLP lenders, approved deals get forwarded to the SBA for a secondary review.

**Step 6: Approval and conditions.** Most approvals come with conditions — additional documentation, clarifications, or structural adjustments the lender needs before closing.

**Step 7: Closing.** All conditions are met, documents are signed, funds are disbursed. The deal is done.

That seven-step sequence is the skeleton of every SBA transaction. The details change based on deal type, loan size, and lender preferences, but the flow is the same.

## Key Terms Every Originator Must Know

You'll hear these terms in every deal conversation. Know them cold.

**SOP (Standard Operating Procedure).** SOP 50 10 is the SBA's master rulebook. It governs eligibility, guarantee percentages, fee structures, documentation requirements, and everything else. It runs over 500 pages. You don't need to memorize it, but you need to know where to find things in it.

**PLP (Preferred Lender Program).** Lenders with delegated authority to approve SBA loans without SBA review.

**DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio).** The ratio of available cash flow to total debt payments. Lenders typically want 1.15x to 1.25x minimum. This is the number one metric in underwriting.

**Equity Injection.** The borrower's cash contribution to the deal. Minimum 10% for acquisitions, often higher in practice.

**Guarantee Fee.** The fee the SBA charges for its guarantee. Paid by the borrower, scaled by loan amount and maturity. Usually rolled into the loan.

**Authorization.** The SBA's formal approval document that outlines the terms and conditions of the guarantee.

**Standby Seller Note.** A seller-financed portion of an acquisition where the seller agrees to defer all payments for a period (typically 24 months or the life of the loan). Can count toward the buyer's equity injection.

**NAICS Code.** The North American Industry Classification System code that identifies the borrower's industry. This determines SBA size standards and eligibility.

**Credit Elsewhere Test.** The SBA requirement that borrowers demonstrate they cannot obtain the loan on reasonable terms without the SBA guarantee. In practice, this is usually handled by the lender as a documentation step.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does the SBA lend money directly to borrowers?
No. The SBA guarantees loans made by approved lenders — banks, credit unions, and CDFIs. The borrower applies through the lender, not through the SBA.

### What's the difference between a PLP lender and a non-PLP lender?
PLP lenders can approve SBA loans in-house without sending the application to the SBA for review. This speeds up the process. Non-PLP lenders must submit approved deals to the SBA for a secondary approval.

### How long does an SBA 7(a) loan take from application to closing?
It varies. PLP deals can close in 30 to 60 days if the package is clean. Non-PLP deals take longer because of the additional SBA review. Complex deals — acquisitions with multiple entities, partner buyouts, real estate components — can take 90 days or more.

### Can startups get SBA 7(a) loans?
Yes, but it's harder. Startups lack operating history and cash flow, which means the lender is relying more heavily on the borrower's industry experience, equity injection, and business plan projections. Some lenders won't do startup deals at all. CDFIs are often a better fit.

### What's the minimum credit score for an SBA loan?
The SBA doesn't set a minimum credit score. Individual lenders do. Most want a FICO of 650 to 680 or higher. Below 620, you're going to have a hard time, but it's not impossible with strong compensating factors.

### How do originators actually earn money in SBA lending?
Through referral fees (also called broker fees or packaging fees) paid by the lender when the deal closes. Fee structures vary by lender and deal size, typically ranging from 0.5% to 1.5% of the loan amount. Some originators also earn through ongoing servicing relationships.

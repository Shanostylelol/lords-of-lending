# SBA Eligibility Deep Dive: Who Really Qualifies?

I think the most expensive mistake an originator can make is spending weeks packaging a deal that was never eligible in the first place. I've seen it happen more times than I can count. Somebody works a transaction for forty hours, gets the borrower excited, gets a lender interested, and then someone at the bank pulls up the SBA's ineligible business list and the whole thing is dead. Done. Forty hours of work, zero revenue, and a borrower who's now frustrated and mistrustful.

Eligibility isn't sexy. Nobody gets into SBA lending because they're fascinated by NAICS codes and size standards. But this is the foundation that everything else sits on, and getting it wrong doesn't just cost you one deal. It costs you credibility with lenders who will remember that you sent them an ineligible package. That sort of thing follows you.

The SBA's eligibility rules are laid out in SOP 50 10, and they're more specific than most people expect. This guide covers what you actually need to know to evaluate eligibility quickly and accurately, before you invest time in a deal that can't work.

---

## Size Standards by NAICS Code

The SBA defines "small business" differently depending on your industry. The definition is tied to your NAICS code — the North American Industry Classification System — which categorizes every business into an industry sector.

For most service businesses, the size standard is based on average annual receipts over the previous five years. The threshold varies but commonly falls between $8 million and $16.5 million, depending on the specific NAICS code. For manufacturing, it's usually based on employee count, with the threshold typically set at 500 or 1,250 employees.

Here's why this matters in practice. A construction company with $30 million in annual revenue might still qualify as "small" under its NAICS code if the threshold for that code is $39.5 million. A software company with $10 million in revenue might be too big if its NAICS code has a $9 million threshold. You cannot assume. You have to look it up.

The SBA maintains a full table of size standards at [sba.gov/size-standards](https://www.sba.gov/size-standards). I think every originator should bookmark this page and check it on every single deal. It takes thirty seconds and it can save you from a catastrophic waste of time.

There's an additional wrinkle. The SBA looks at the borrower's affiliates when determining size. If your borrower owns 30% of one business and 50% of another, those businesses' revenues or employees get added together for size standard purposes. I've seen deals where the target business was well under the size threshold, but the borrower's ownership in other companies pushed the combined entity over the limit. That's an eligibility problem that only surfaces if you ask the right questions early.

## The "Credit Elsewhere" Test

This one confuses a lot of people, so I want to be clear about what it is and what it isn't.

The SBA requires that borrowers demonstrate they cannot obtain the loan on "reasonable terms" without the SBA guarantee. This is called the credit elsewhere test, and it's a formal requirement of the program. The logic is straightforward: the SBA guarantee is a taxpayer-backed benefit, and it's intended for businesses that genuinely need it to access credit.

In practice, here's what this looks like. The lender documents that the borrower either applied and was declined for conventional financing, or that the terms of conventional financing (higher down payment, shorter term, higher rate, personal collateral requirements) would be unreasonable for the borrower's situation. Most PLP lenders handle this as a documentation step in their credit memo. They check a box, write a brief justification, and move on.

I think the key thing for originators to understand is that the credit elsewhere test is almost never the reason a deal gets declined. It's a regulatory requirement that lenders are experienced at satisfying. But you should know it exists, because occasionally a borrower with strong financials and extensive collateral will be told they don't actually need an SBA loan — they could get conventional financing on reasonable terms. In those cases, the lender may push them toward a conventional product, which is fine for the borrower but means no SBA guarantee fee for the lender and potentially a different fee structure for you.

## Eligible vs. Ineligible Businesses: The SBA's Actual List

This is where originators need to pay close attention. The SBA maintains a specific list of business types that are not eligible for 7(a) financing. Some of these are obvious. Some are not.

### Ineligible Business Types

- **Passive real estate investment.** Buying a property to rent it out is not an SBA-eligible purpose. Owner-occupied real estate where the business uses at least 51% of the space is eligible. Pure investment real estate is not.
- **Gambling operations.** Casinos, racetracks, and businesses where more than one-third of revenue comes from gambling activity.
- **Lending and investment businesses.** Banks, finance companies, life insurance companies, and businesses primarily engaged in lending or investment.
- **Pyramid sales / multi-level marketing.** The SBA explicitly excludes these.
- **Speculative businesses.** Businesses that derive revenue primarily from speculation rather than from providing goods or services.
- **Government-owned entities.** Businesses owned by government entities are ineligible.
- **Businesses engaged in illegal activity.** This includes cannabis businesses at the federal level, regardless of state legality.
- **Businesses previously non-compliant with government loans.** If the borrower defaulted on a prior federal loan, that's a significant issue.
- **Religious organizations.** Nonprofits and religious organizations are ineligible (though for-profit businesses owned by religious individuals are fine).
- **Businesses with the principal purpose of political or lobbying activity.**

### Commonly Misunderstood Eligibility

Some business types fall into gray areas where originators make incorrect assumptions:

**Cannabis-adjacent businesses.** The business itself cannot touch cannabis, but a landlord renting space to a cannabis tenant might be eligible if the cannabis-related income is below certain thresholds. This is a moving target and you need to check the current SOP language.

**Alcohol-related businesses.** Bars, breweries, and liquor stores are generally eligible. The SBA removed most alcohol restrictions years ago, but some lenders still won't do them based on their own credit policies.

**Adult entertainment.** The SBA has historically excluded businesses of a "prurient sexual nature." In practice, this means adult bookstores, strip clubs, and similar establishments. Standard nightclubs and entertainment venues are fine.

**Nonprofits.** Nonprofits are ineligible, but this catches people off guard when a borrower has a for-profit business that also has a nonprofit affiliate. The for-profit entity can still qualify.

The full ineligible list is in SOP 50 10. If you're ever uncertain about a specific business type, check the SOP before you invest time. A five-minute search beats a five-week dead end.

## Character Requirements

Every individual who owns 20% or more of the business must complete SBA Form 912, the Statement of Personal History. This is the SBA's character review, and it covers criminal history, prior government loan defaults, and other background issues.

Here's what triggers additional scrutiny or potential disqualification:

- **Felony convictions.** Certain felonies within specific timeframes can disqualify a borrower. The SBA has updated its policies over the years, and the current standards are more specific than a blanket exclusion. Crimes involving fraud or financial dishonesty are treated more severely than other offenses.
- **Prior SBA loan defaults.** If the borrower defaulted on a prior SBA loan and the government took a loss, that's a serious red flag. It doesn't automatically disqualify them, but the borrower will need to demonstrate what changed and may need to make restitution.
- **Incarceration.** Individuals currently incarcerated, on probation, or on parole are generally ineligible.
- **Debarment.** Anyone debarred from government contracting or programs is ineligible.

I think the important thing for originators is to ask these questions early. Not in a judgmental way, but directly. "Have you ever been convicted of a felony? Have you ever defaulted on a government loan?" If the answer is yes, it doesn't necessarily kill the deal, but you need to know before you're in front of a lender. Surprises on the 912 are among the most embarrassing and avoidable problems in this business.

## Special Eligibility Rules

### Franchises

Franchise businesses are eligible for SBA financing, but only if the franchise is listed on the SBA Franchise Directory. The SBA reviews franchise agreements to ensure they don't contain provisions that would give the franchisor too much control over the business (which would make the franchisee something other than a truly independent small business).

If your borrower wants to buy a franchise, the first thing you check is whether that franchise is on the SBA directory. If it's not, the deal stops until it is. Some franchisors have never applied for SBA listing, and the process takes time.

### Startups

Startups are eligible for SBA 7(a) loans. This surprises people because there's a widespread misconception that you need an existing business to qualify. That said, startup deals are harder to close because there's no operating history or cash flow to underwrite. Lenders evaluate startup deals based on the borrower's industry experience, equity injection, business plan projections, and personal financial strength.

If you want to understand why startups face higher hurdles despite being eligible, our article on [Why Startups Get Denied Loans and What to Do Instead](/why-startups-get-denied-loans-and-what-to-do-instead) covers the practical reality.

### Change of Ownership

Business acquisitions are one of the most common uses of the 7(a) program. Eligibility for change-of-ownership deals includes all the standard requirements plus additional considerations: the purchase price must be supported by a business valuation, the buyer must demonstrate relevant experience (or bring in management that does), and the equity injection requirement applies.

### Partner Buyouts

An existing owner buying out a partner is an eligible use of 7(a) proceeds. The mechanics are similar to an acquisition, with the valuation based on the departing partner's equity stake and the remaining owner's ability to service the debt.

## Common Eligibility Traps Originators Miss

I've been spotting issues in SBA deals for years, and these are the eligibility traps that catch originators over and over again.

**Affiliate size aggregation.** The borrower's target business is small, but the borrower owns pieces of other businesses that push the aggregate over the size standard. You have to ask about ownership in other entities at intake. Every time.

**Ineligible use of proceeds mixed with eligible.** A borrower wants to buy a business and also acquire an investment property. The business acquisition is eligible. The investment property is not. If the proceeds are commingled, the entire loan can be flagged. Keep the uses clean and separated.

**Franchise not on the SBA directory.** The borrower assumes their franchise is approved because it's a well-known brand. It's not on the directory. Deal stalls.

**Cannabis adjacency.** The borrower's business is a property management company. One of the tenants is a dispensary. Now you've got a cannabis issue that needs to be evaluated under the current SOP rules. If you didn't ask about it at intake, you find out during underwriting, and the deal potentially dies.

**Change in ownership structure mid-process.** The borrower decides to add a partner after submitting the package. Now the new partner needs to be evaluated — credit check, 912, PFS, the whole thing. That delays the deal by weeks and may change the eligibility picture entirely.

**Prior government loan defaults.** The borrower mentions, casually, three weeks into the process, that they had an SBA loan on a previous business that didn't work out. If there was a loss to the government, that's a problem you should have known about on day one. If an eligibility issue or any other factor leads to a denial, our guide on [what to do when your SBA loan gets denied](/sba-loan-denied) covers the recovery path — including when to fix the issue and resubmit versus when to try a different approach.

The common thread here is intake discipline. If you're asking the right questions in the first conversation — about affiliates, about criminal history, about prior government loans, about the specific business type, about all ownership interests — you catch these issues before they become expensive problems. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can a business with foreign ownership get an SBA loan?
The SBA requires that the business be at least 51% owned and controlled by U.S. citizens or lawful permanent residents. If foreign nationals hold a minority stake, it may still be eligible depending on the specifics, but majority foreign ownership is disqualifying.

### Are online businesses eligible for SBA loans?
Yes. E-commerce companies, SaaS businesses, and other online-first companies are eligible as long as they meet all other SBA requirements. The business must be for-profit, meet size standards, and operate legally.

### Can I get an SBA loan if I already have one?
Yes. There's no rule limiting a borrower to one SBA loan. However, the total guaranteed amount across all SBA loans for one borrower is capped at $5 million. If you have a $3 million SBA loan outstanding, you could potentially get up to $2 million more.

### What happens if the SBA determines a business was ineligible after the loan closes?
If the lender didn't follow SBA rules in verifying eligibility, the SBA can deny the guarantee. This means the lender eats the entire loss if the borrower defaults. This is why lenders are careful about eligibility — they're protecting their own guarantee.

### How do I check if a specific business type is eligible?
Start with the ineligible business list in SOP 50 10. If the business type isn't explicitly listed as ineligible, it's likely eligible. For gray areas, contact the SBA's district office or consult with a PLP lender who can give you a definitive answer.

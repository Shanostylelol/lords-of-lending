# SBA Fee Structures Explained

I think one of the most common places originators lose credibility is when a borrower asks "how much is this going to cost me?" and the originator can't give a clear answer. SBA fee structures aren't complicated once you learn them, but they're layered. There's the SBA guarantee fee, the lender's closing costs, packaging fees, and the originator's own compensation. If you can't explain each piece and how they fit together, the borrower fills that gap with suspicion.

The good news is that the SBA regulates most of the fee structure. There are caps, formulas, and rules that keep things predictable. But the details matter, and I've seen originators lose deals — or worse, lose lender relationships — because they quoted fees incorrectly or failed to disclose costs the borrower didn't expect.

This guide breaks down every fee an originator needs to understand, with the math worked out so you can explain it clearly to borrowers and structure your own compensation with confidence.

## SBA Guarantee Fee Tables

The SBA charges a guarantee fee on every 7(a) loan. This fee is how the SBA funds the guarantee program — the borrower pays for the government's backing. The fee is calculated based on the guaranteed portion of the loan, not the full loan amount, and it scales with loan size and maturity.

Here are the current guarantee fee rates:

### Loans with maturity of 12 months or less

| Loan Amount | Guarantee Fee |
|---|---|
| Up to $1,000,000 | 0.00% |
| $1,000,001 – $5,000,000 | 0.00% |

Short-term loans under 12 months currently carry no guarantee fee.

### Loans with maturity greater than 12 months

| Guaranteed Portion | Upfront Guarantee Fee | Annual Servicing Fee |
|---|---|---|
| $150,000 or less | 2.0% of guaranteed portion | 0.55% |
| $150,001 – $700,000 | 3.0% of guaranteed portion | 0.55% |
| $700,001 – $1,000,000 | 3.5% of guaranteed portion | 0.55% |
| $1,000,001 – $5,000,000 | 3.75% of guaranteed portion | 0.55% |

For loans exceeding $1 million, an additional 0.25% fee applies to the guaranteed portion above $1 million.

These fees change periodically — the SBA has temporarily reduced or waived fees in certain fiscal years as part of stimulus efforts — so always verify the current rates against the most recent SBA notice. But the table above represents the standard structure you'll encounter on most deals.

The guarantee fee is a one-time upfront charge, typically rolled into the loan amount so the borrower doesn't have to pay it out of pocket at closing. The annual servicing fee is ongoing and usually built into the lender's servicing costs.

For a full breakdown of the 7(a) program including rate structures and terms, see our [Complete Guide to SBA 7(a) Loans](/complete-guide-sba-7a-loans).

## Packaging Fees

Packaging fees are what the lender charges the borrower for assembling, processing, and underwriting the SBA loan package. These are separate from the SBA guarantee fee and are not standardized by the SBA — lenders set their own packaging fees within reason.

Typical packaging fees range from $2,500 to $5,000 for smaller deals and can reach $7,500 or more on larger, complex transactions. Some lenders charge a flat fee, others charge a percentage of the loan amount (usually 1% to 2%), and some combine both.

What the SBA does regulate: the total fees charged to the borrower must be "reasonable," and certain fees are explicitly prohibited. Lenders cannot charge excessive prepayment penalties (the SBA limits prepayment penalties to the first three years of the loan term), and they cannot charge fees that duplicate the SBA guarantee fee.

As an originator, knowing the lender's fee structure before you quote a deal to a borrower is critical. I've seen situations where the originator promised the borrower one set of closing costs, the lender's actual fees were higher, and the borrower felt misled. That sort of thing damages the relationship and sometimes kills the deal at the finish line.

## Closing Costs Breakdown

Beyond the guarantee fee and packaging fee, borrowers pay a range of closing costs that mirror what you'd see on a conventional commercial loan. These include:

- **Appraisal fees:** Required for real estate transactions and sometimes for business valuations on acquisitions. Typically $2,500 to $5,000 for real estate; $3,000 to $10,000 for business valuations, depending on complexity.
- **Environmental review:** Phase I environmental assessments run $2,000 to $4,000 and are required for any deal involving real estate.
- **Title insurance and title search:** Required for real estate transactions. Cost varies by location and property value.
- **Legal fees:** Both the borrower and the lender typically have legal counsel at closing. Borrower-side legal fees range from $2,000 to $5,000 on straightforward deals and can be significantly higher on complex acquisitions.
- **UCC filing fees:** Nominal cost for filing liens on business assets.
- **Credit report fees, flood certification, and other minor costs:** Usually a few hundred dollars combined.

The total closing cost picture on an SBA deal typically runs 2% to 5% of the loan amount, all-in. On smaller deals, the percentage skews higher because many costs (appraisals, legal fees, environmental) are fixed regardless of loan size.

I think the best practice for originators is to give borrowers a realistic range early in the process. Not an exact number — that's impossible before you know the full scope — but a range that prepares them for the reality. Nothing erodes trust faster than a borrower who budgeted $5,000 in closing costs and gets hit with a $15,000 bill at the table.

## Broker and Referral Fees: How Originators Earn

This is the section most new originators want to get to, and I understand why. How do you actually get paid?

The standard compensation model for independent SBA loan originators (brokers, referral agents, packaging agents) works like this: the lender pays you a referral fee when the deal closes. This fee comes from the lender's revenue on the transaction, not from the borrower. The borrower should not be paying a separate broker fee on top of the lender's costs — that's a compliance issue and a relationship-killer.

Typical referral fee structures:

| Loan Amount | Typical Referral Fee Range |
|---|---|
| Under $350,000 | 0.50% – 1.00% |
| $350,000 – $1,000,000 | 0.75% – 1.25% |
| $1,000,001 – $2,000,000 | 1.00% – 1.50% |
| $2,000,001 – $5,000,000 | 0.75% – 1.25% |

These ranges are approximate. Every lender sets its own referral fee schedule, and the actual percentage depends on the lender's appetite for the deal, the complexity of the transaction, your relationship with the lender, and the volume you bring.

On a $1 million deal at a 1% referral fee, you're looking at $10,000. On a $3 million deal at 1.25%, that's $37,500. The math works, but only if you're closing deals consistently. An originator who closes one deal every three months at $750,000 average is earning roughly $22,500 to $37,500 per year in referral fees. An originator who closes two deals per month at that average is in a completely different economic position.

Some things to know about referral fee mechanics:

- **Fees are paid at closing.** Not at submission, not at approval. If the deal doesn't close, you don't get paid. This is why deal qualification and time management matter so much.
- **Written agreements are essential.** Always have a signed referral agreement with the lender before you submit a deal. I've seen disputes over fees that could have been avoided with a one-page agreement.
- **Disclosure requirements.** The SBA requires that all fees paid in connection with the loan be disclosed to the borrower. Your referral fee should appear on the closing documents.
- **Volume bonuses.** Some lenders offer higher referral percentages to originators who bring consistent deal flow. Building a relationship with two or three lenders and delivering quality packages consistently can move your fee percentage up over time.

For more detail on referral fee models and how to structure your compensation, see our article on [SBA Referral Fee Structures](/sba-referral-fee-structures).

## How Fees Are Typically Rolled Into the Loan

One of the advantages of SBA lending for borrowers is that many of the upfront costs can be financed — rolled into the loan amount rather than paid out of pocket at closing.

The SBA guarantee fee is almost always rolled in. This is standard practice and expected by lenders. Most closing costs can also be included in the use-of-proceeds breakdown, as long as the total loan amount (including rolled-in fees) stays within the $5 million cap and the deal's DSCR still works with the higher loan balance.

Here's the trade-off borrowers need to understand: rolling fees into the loan reduces your upfront cash requirement but increases the loan balance, which means higher monthly payments and more total interest paid over the life of the loan. On a 10-year term, rolling $25,000 in fees into a loan at 10.5% adds roughly $340 per month to the payment and costs about $15,800 in additional interest over the life of the loan.

For borrowers who are tight on cash for equity injection, rolling fees in makes sense because it preserves capital for the business. For borrowers with ample liquidity, paying fees out of pocket and keeping the loan balance lower can save meaningful money over time. I think a good originator presents both options and lets the borrower decide based on their situation.

## Total Cost Example: $750,000 Business Acquisition

Let me walk through a realistic fee scenario to show how everything adds up. This is a $750,000 business acquisition with a 10-year term.

**The deal:**
- Purchase price: $750,000
- SBA 7(a) loan: $675,000 (borrower putting 10% down = $75,000 equity injection)
- Guarantee percentage: 75% = $506,250 guaranteed portion
- Loan term: 10 years
- Interest rate: Prime + 2.75% = 10.25%

**Fee breakdown:**

| Fee | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| SBA Guarantee Fee | $17,719 | 3.5% of $506,250 guaranteed portion |
| Packaging Fee | $3,500 | Lender's flat fee |
| Business Valuation | $5,000 | Required for acquisition |
| Legal Fees (borrower side) | $3,500 | Closing attorney |
| Environmental Review | $0 | No real estate in this deal |
| Title/UCC Filing | $500 | Lien filing costs |
| Credit Report & Misc | $300 | Standard processing |
| **Total Fees** | **$30,519** | **~4.5% of loan amount** |

If the borrower rolls all fees into the loan, the total financed amount becomes approximately $705,519. The monthly payment on $705,519 at 10.25% over 10 years is roughly $9,455 per month, versus $9,050 per month if fees were paid out of pocket. That's about $400 per month in additional debt service, or $48,600 over the life of the loan.

**The originator's fee on this deal:** At 1% referral, that's $6,750. At 1.25%, that's $8,438. Paid at closing.

This is the kind of math you need to be able to do on the fly. Not with perfect precision — the numbers will shift during underwriting — but close enough to set accurate expectations with the borrower and evaluate whether the deal works financially.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can the SBA guarantee fee be waived?
In certain fiscal years, the SBA has waived or reduced guarantee fees as part of economic stimulus programs. During normal periods, the fee applies to all loans above 12 months in maturity. Always check the current SBA notice for the fiscal year you're originating in.

### Who pays the originator's referral fee — the borrower or the lender?
The lender pays the referral fee out of their revenue on the transaction. Borrowers should not be charged a separate broker fee. All fees must be disclosed on the closing documents.

### Are SBA loan fees tax deductible?
Generally, yes. The guarantee fee and other loan-related costs are typically deductible as business expenses, though the deduction may need to be amortized over the life of the loan. Borrowers should consult their tax advisor for specifics.

### What's the difference between the guarantee fee and the annual servicing fee?
The guarantee fee is a one-time upfront charge paid at closing. The annual servicing fee (0.55%) is an ongoing charge that the lender pays to the SBA each year, usually absorbed into the lender's servicing costs rather than charged separately to the borrower.

### Can I charge the borrower directly for my services?
This is a gray area that varies by state and by lender policy. The SBA requires that all compensation be disclosed and reasonable. Most originators are paid by the lender through referral agreements. Charging the borrower directly (separate from lender fees) can create compliance issues and damage trust. I'd recommend sticking to the lender-paid referral model.
